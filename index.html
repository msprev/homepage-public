I am Senior Lecturer in Philosophy at the University of Edinburgh.

My primary research interests are in philosophy of mind, philosophy of science, metaphysics, and philosophy of language. I am interested in how these issues in these areas intersect with cognitive science.

Recently, I have become interested in building bridges between current philosophical work and cognition and other humanities

I did my Ph.D. in 2006 with Professor Peter Lipton at the Department History and Philosophy of Science at the University of Cambridge. I worked as a Junior Research Fellow and Leverhulme Early Career Fellow in Cambridge. Since 2011, I have been at the University of Edinburgh. I have been a visitor at University of Pittsburgh, University of Notre Dame, and University of Florence.

I am current Director of Knowledge Exchange for PPLS as well as Deputy Director for Research.

History of Distributed Cognition

Exploring historical roots
  • AHRC funded project

Where is my mind? Distributed cognition is an umbrella term for the (initially rather fuzzy) idea that external objects, like iPhones, are in some sense part of our mental lives. Excellent philosophical work has been done in recent years in making this more precise. As one might expect, that position is complex: there are many incompatible ways fleshing out the basic idea of distributed cognition. But here’s thought. To date, most potential cases of distributed cognition come from modern technology (iPhones, computers, warships, and so on). But the basic idea – that the external objects may do mental work – is an old one. How was human cognition distributed in different historical times? How did people in those times think about cognition and the boundaries of the mind? What might have caused them to assume an internal versus distributed mental life?

We are trying to answer these questions. Together with Douglas Cairns (PI, Classics), Miranda Anderson (English Literature) and Mike Wheeler (Philosophy), we have an Eidyn-hosted £600,000 AHRC grant, A History of Distributed Cognition. What is exciting about this project is that there is potential for two-way exchange between the humanities and sciences. The sciences gain access to new paradigm cases of distributed cognition plus an appreciation of how their current controversy has been historically conditioned. The humanities gains a conceptual framework that throws new light on old historical episodes and promises a unification of material culture with history of ideas.

Major outputs include online seminars that provide a state-of-the-art introduction to distributed cognition for a wide audience of researchers. Our next major output is a big one: a set of four volumes (A History of Distributed Cognition), that aims to answer the research questions above. We have recruited a large number of scholars across the humanities for these volumes, and we organised intensive workshops to iterate on chapter drafts. Regarding intellectual exchange in the other direction, Mike Wheeler and myself are preparing a paper that will introduce some lessons learned so far from the project to the philosophy and psychology community. We hope our project will stimulate other researchers, in the humanities and sciences, to explore the vast and interesting research topics opened up by seeing the past through the theoretical framework of distributed cognition.

Mind Network

A network for philosophy of mind and cognitive science

The Mind Network is a community of UK researchers in philosophy of mind and cognitive science. We meet twice a year. We present papers, exchange ideas, and have a jolly good time. Graduate students and new arrivals to the UK community are particularly welcome. Subscribe to our mailing list below to join us. I am one of the founders and organisers.
