Asking for a letter of reference

25 April 2017
 pdf

If you want the best possible letter, it is rational to make the referee’s job easier.

Basic etiquette

  • Email politely to ask if the referee would be willing to write a reference
  • If this is the first time the referee writes, give at least 2 weeks’ notice to compose a letter
  • Clearly state for what, to whom, and when, the reference is due
  • Once a letter is written, sending it again, or updating it, is relatively quick
  • If you are applying for many positions, consider using Interfolio, Academic Applications Online, or similar
  • Send all the information about (a) you and (b) the job needed to write the reference
  • Send updates for the next letter
  • Letters of reference lose their value if they are not confidential

What to send

  1. Information about the position(s) you are applying for, including the job specification and further particulars
  2. Your current CV
  3. A list of items that the referee may wish to include in the reference
    • Be organised (e.g. publications, teaching, conference papers, knowledge exchange, etc.)
    • Have relevant details for each item (where, when, what, etc.)
    • Explain briefly why an item should impress (e.g. excellent scores, prize-winning, journal’s acceptance rate)
  4. Samples of work
    • Your most recent recent and significant papers
    • If you are a student, as much of your PhD/MSc thesis as possible and a time-line for completion
  5. For each sample of work
    • Include a clear abstract
    • For draft papers, say where you intend to submit them

Nit picking

  • Send all the information to the referee in one email
  • Use PDFs in preference to other document formats
  • Highlight relevant information in the documents (e.g. deadlines, instructions to referees, selection criteria in a job specification)
