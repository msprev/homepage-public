How to write a first class dissertation

25 April 2017
 pdf

A first class dissertation should…

  1. Have a claim
  2. Right size of claim
  3. Rigorous argument for your claim
  4. Clarity
  5. Structure — syntax should mirror semantics
  6. Tight focus
  7. Make clear your original contribution

A first class dissertation needn’t be…

  1. Be a magnum opus
  2. Your last word on the topic
  3. Describe every possible view in the field
  4. Conclusively establish your claim
  5. Solve a major problem (e.g. the mind-body problem, external world scepticism)

How do I write a first class dissertation?

  1. Find a general area you like (e.g. phenomenal consciousness)
  2. Select one article/book chapter in that area that you find fascinating (e.g. Block (2009))
  3. Summarise Block (2009) carefully in your own words, paying attention to whether each step in the argument follows from the previous
  4. Look for weaknesses in Block (2009)’s argument
  5. Summarise how Block (2009)’s argument should be corrected. Focus on the following:
    • Which new resources do you need to draw on?
    • Which alternative conclusions follow?
    • Which objections can be raised to your proposal?

You have a first class dissertation!

How can I write 8,000 words?

  • Introduction
    • What is your claim, the outline of your argument? (500 words)
  • Chapter 1:
    • Why is your claim important? What are the pay-offs? (1,000 words)
  • Chapter 2
    • Careful and charitable summary of X in your own words (2,000 words)
  • Chapter 3
    • Your rigorous criticism of X (2,000 words)
  • Chapter 4
    • How X should be corrected, associated costs, consequences for views that use X, possible objections (2,000 words)
  • Conclusion (500 words)

Milestones for UG dissertations

Year 3

  1. End Y3: meet supervisor & find general topic
  2. Summer: reading on topic
  3. Start Y4: find 1 article/chapter to focus

Year 4, Semester 1

  • Start: meet with supervisor & agree plan
  • Middle: first draft of 2 chapters
  • End: polished draft of 2 chapters

Year 4, Semester 2

  • Start: first draft of entire dissertation
  • Middle: polished draft of entire dissertation
  • End: revisit, revise, and submit dissertation

Tricks of the pros

  • ‘A dissertation in philosophy is a story … like all good stories, it only includes what is essential to the story’ — Robert Paul Wolff’s astute advice that applies just as well to UG work as well as PhD theses: http://goo.gl/7Y4c4

  • ‘Be concise, but explain yourself fully’ — Jim Pryor with an excellent 3-stage plan for writing philosophy: http://goo.gl/e6tyz

  • ‘Style is the feather in the arrow, not the feather in the cap’ — Peter Lipton has some wonderful and concise writing advice: http://goo.gl/WzVxz

  • ‘Read your work aloud. … Be firm: take your prose to the gym, and keep working at it until the bones and sinews show through!’ — Peter Smith, previously editor of Analysis, with some fantastic advice: — http://goo.gl/0x97u

  • ‘What is an argument?’ – Jim Pryor’s guide is essential reading for anyone writing philosophy; it contains a useful lexicon of philosophical terms and a taxonomy of good and bad arguments http://goo.gl/N80eW
